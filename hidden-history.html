Hidden History of NYC

The city of New York, which is recognized as a city of opportunities, is frequently well-known for its significant development and social diversity, as well as the number of stories that have been told during its history of five hundred years. There is a significant portion of the city's history that has been buried beneath the surface of the city's landscape, and the historical events have experienced a decline in visibility as time has progressed. The group of people who originally inhabited this area, as well as the African community, who made significant contributions to this city but have been mostly forgotten. Through my visits to the "National Museum of the American Indian" and the "African Burial Ground National Monument," I have been able to acquire and gain a more comprehensive understanding of the contributions that they have made. For the aim of better organizing this article and providing a more in-depth understanding of the ways in which colonialism and racial inequality have impacted the city and our community, I will be using a few readings, articles and academic resources published by universities, and well-known authors to better structure this article.

Upon entering the National Museum of the American Indian, which is located in lower Manhattan, I was surrounded by the powerful energy of the rich culture and history of the Native American tribes, particularly the Lenape community, which had resided in the New York City area for thousands of years. It is not only the social and cultural wealth of Native American life that is revealed by the museum, but also the fact and manner in which their past has been commercialized throughout the course of time. In the past few decades, especially during the world war periods, Native American culture was being repackaged for commercial consumption and being used to raise funds with their "Native American Spirit" which may track consumer's consumption desire due to its scarcity in a white dominant society. As observed by Navratil, a student at the City University of New York. "A substantial number and variety of gallery and museum shows staged in New York City in this period raised awareness of and appreciation for indigenous art … leading to wider incorporation of Native American production into modern U.S. domestic interiors and to take-offs on Native American motifs and materials in furniture and fashion design." (Navratil, p. 2). The real cultural and spiritual importance of Native American artifacts and arts was frequently buried as a result of these commercial movements, which reduced them to simple symbols for advertising commercial products and being used in the fashion industry. Another historian, Levine, draws attention to the fact that the Lenape people are actually historically connected to a few of the most famous landmarks in New York City located in lower Manhattan. According to the introduction paragraph of Levine (2018), "From Bowling Green to Broadway, although the Lenape strove to 'walk so gently on the earth,' without leaving an impact on the land, they influenced the city's physical geography in ways we can see and feel today" (Levine 2018 Intro Paragraph)" The National Museum of American Indian illustrates the difficulties and challenges that Native American communities have faced in the past and continue to face today. One example of this is the migration of Lenape towns in the New York area that are forced to leave the island of Manhattan and move somewhere else. Since the settlement of colonizers from Europe to North America during the 17th century, the life of Lenape people have been gradually being pressured. Lenape's land has been taken over, and their community are forced to move elsewhere due to the sign of unequal treaties with the Europeans and the disease such as smallpox from Europe continent. Additionally, the museum highlights the Native American communities' unique sovereignty and cultural identity. Using the Lenape exhibit as an example, it has created significant cultural ties to this region such as their sense of sovereignty with the land and their own cultural identity including their own language and artistic artifact particularly to the island of Manhattan.

My connection to the Native American community has been deeply enhanced after my visit to the National Museum of the American Indian located in Alexander Hamilton U.S. Custom House in Lower Manhattan. The museum thoughtfully planned the native american exhibits and brought to life their vibrant culture histories and contribution to this land. Additionally, my visit has also caused me to reevaluate and reflect on the ways in which the development of New York City, a global hub for modernity and progresses that has frequently obscured the indigenous past.

As an international student studying in the United States who comes from a country with a single ethnic group. My understanding of the concept of racism was limited and at some times easily to be confused. But over the course of my history study, I have gradually gained a deeper understanding of the racial injustices problems that have occurred throughout the history of the African American community and by visiting the African Burial Ground National Monument, I have further expanded my knowledge and perspective on these critical issues.

In terms of racism, the achievements made by African populations in New York have been mostly ignored and forgotten by today's U.S. society where white population forms up around seventy five percent of the total population. One of the arguments put forward by Blakey, an American Anthropologist, is that "At New York's eighteenth-century African Burial Ground the careful burial of the dead is obvious evidence of Africans' continued belief in their own humanity against European objections (Blakey, p. S187)" This memorial is not merely a tourist attraction or a basic structure; rather, it is a monument that brings to light the sad history of racism directed toward African communities. It serves more as a reminder and a symbolic representation of those African Americans who have made contributions to the city and to the United States of America as a whole. By understanding the importance of African Burial Ground, it's clear that acknowledging such histories helps combat the invisibility and marginalization endured by African populations. It is not only about preserving memories, but also about actively engaging with past injustices. The African Burial Ground stands as a testament to the resilience of a community that refused to let their narratives be erased. With proper recognition and scholarly attention, these histories can inform contemporary discussions on race, equity, and social justice. Just as Blakey stated, "By operating consistently with principles of informed consent, the African Burial Ground Project, 1992–2009, was the first ethical bioarchaeological project in which scientific methods and human rights were made complementary. (Blakey, p. S190)

It is plausible that the incorporation of these historical narratives into contemporary education and urban design could lead to a greater level of societal awareness and inclusivity, by guaranteeing that the voices of underrepresented communities are not wiped or disregarded from the annals of history. The history of enslaved Africans and native Lenape, who made significant contributions to today's New York City but have been forgotten over the course of time, was brought to light by institutions such as these museums. Just like Peterson from Black Gotham once said, "these understandings led them to the unswerving conviction that beyond cultural difference lay the universal, ethical value of character that all human beings needed to cultivate (Peterson, p.43).By combining those historical narratives into engagement topics and educational articles, our society can form a more inclusive public memory of the past. By doing so is not only acknowledging the profound and contribution of these communities to New York City's cultural fabric, but also giving us power to challenge the mistaken myth, adapt with the uncomfortable truth from the past, and strive towards a more inclusive and equal society in nowadays' rapidly changing world.
