Hidden History of NYC

The city of New York, which is recognized as a city of opportunities, is well-known for its significant development and social diversity, as well as the number of stories that have been told during its history of five hundred years. There is a significant portion of the city's history that has been buried beneath the surface of its landscape; numerous significant historical events have declined in visibility and awareness as time has progressed. Indigenous groups who originally inhabited this land, as well as the African-American community whose presence was so vital to the growth of the city have been mostly erased. Through my visits to the National Museum of the American Indian and the African Burial Ground National Monument, I have been able to acquire and gain a more comprehensive understanding of the contributions of these groups. I will be analyzing anthropologist Michael Blakey’s “Archaeology Under the Building Light of Race” and Emily Schuchardt Navratil’s “Native American Chic”, in order to provide a more in-depth understanding of the ways in which colonialism and racial inequality have impacted the city and our community.

Upon entering the National Museum of the American Indian in lower Manhattan, I was surrounded by the powerful energy of the rich culture and history of the Native American tribes. The museum is located in what was formerly the Alexander Hamilton U.S. Custom House, ironically situated in that Indigenous communities are the original inhabitants of this land. Particularly, the museum focused on the Lenape community, which had resided in the New York City area for thousands of years. Not only is the social and cultural wealth of Native American life revealed by the museum, but also the fact and manner in which their past has been commercialized throughout the course of time. In the past few decades, especially during the world war periods, Native American culture was repackaged for commercial consumption. Their culture was leveraged to raise funds through "Native American Spirit", which may track consumer's consumption desire due to its scarcity in a white dominant society. As observed by Navratil, a scholar at the City University of New York: "A substantial number and variety of gallery and museum shows staged in New York City in this period raised awareness of and appreciation for indigenous art … leading to wider incorporation of Native American production into modern U.S. domestic interiors and to take-offs on Native American motifs and materials in furniture and fashion design" (Navratil 2). The real cultural and spiritual importance of Native American artifacts and arts was frequently buried as a result of these commercial movements, reducing them to simple symbols for advertising commercial products in the fashion industry. Historian, Lucie Levine, draws attention to the fact that the Lenape people are actually historically connected to a few of the most famous landmarks in New York City located in lower Manhattan. Their impact can be felt in the way the city took structure, "from Bowling Green to Broadway, although the Lenape strove to ‘walk so gently on the earth,’ … they influenced the city’s physical geography in ways we can see and feel today” (Levine 1). The National Museum of American Indian illustrates the difficulties and challenges that Native American communities have faced in the past and continue to face today. In the 17th century, the Lenape people were forced to leave the island of Manhattan and move to neighboring areas. Since the settlement of colonizers from Europe to North America during the 17th century, the Lenape people have been gradually scratched out of history, through their forced migration. Lenape land has been taken over through unequal treaties with the Europeans and diseases such as smallpox spread from Europe continent. Using the Lenape exhibit, the National Museum of American Indian has created significant cultural ties to this region such as a sense of sovereignty with the land, and their own cultural identity including language and artistic artifact particular to the island of Manhattan.

My connection to the Native American community has been deeply enhanced after my visit to the National Museum of the American Indian. The museum thoughtfully planned Native American exhibits and brought to life their vibrant culture histories and contribution to this land. Additionally, my visit has also allowed me to reevaluate and reflect on the ways in which the development of New York City, a global hub for modernity and progresses, has frequently obscured the indigenous past.

As an international student studying in the United States, coming from a country with a single ethnic group, my understanding of the concept of racism was limited and at times and easily confused. Over the course of my time in New York in conjunction with my studies, I have been exposed to more of the impacts of racial injustice, specifically in the United States. Similar to my experience with the National Museum of the American Indian, I have gradually gained a deeper understanding of racial injustices faced by the African American community. After visiting the African Burial Ground National Monument, I have further expanded my knowledge and perspective on these critical issues.

The achievements made by African populations in New York have been mostly ignored and forgotten by contemporary U.S. history. This whitewashing has occurred because of “othering” of a minority group, where the US white population forms up around seventy five percent of the country. One of the arguments put forward by Blakey, an American Anthropologist, is that “At New York’s eighteenth-century African Burial Ground the careful burial of the dead is obvious evidence of Africans’ continued belief in their own humanity against European objections” (Blakey S187). This memorial is not merely a tourist attraction or a basic structure; rather, it is a monument that brings to light the sad history of racism directed toward African communities. It serves more as a reminder and a symbolic representation of those African Americans who have made contributions to the city and to the United States of America as a whole. By understanding the importance of the African Burial Ground, it’s clear that acknowledging such histories helps combat the invisibility and marginalization endured by African populations. It is not only about preserving memories, but also about actively engaging with past injustices. The African Burial Ground stands as a testament to the resilience of a community that refused to let their narratives be erased. With proper recognition and scholarly attention, these histories can inform contemporary discussions on race, equity, and social justice. Just as Blakey demonstrates, “by operating consistently with principles of informed consent, the African Burial Ground Project, 1992–2009, was the first ethical bioarchaeological project in which scientific methods and human rights were made complementary (Blakey S190)”. That it often discourses surrounding race are not taken seriously or being delegitimized; in order to proactively combat this, Blakey shows the significance of science and fighting against injustice working in tandem with one another.

The incorporation of the historical narratives of the Lenape and African American communities into contemporary education will allow a greater level of societal awareness and inclusivity. The voices and impact of these underrepresented groups are felt throughout the culture of New York City, as literally as its urban design, and should not be wiped or disregarded from the course of history. Author of Black Gotham, Carla Peterson, sheds light on the spirit and resilience of historically erased communities’ lasting impacts. Only when viewing history intersectionality, “beyond cultural difference lay the universal, ethical value of character that all human beings needed to cultivate (Peterson 43). The history of enslaved Africans and Native Lenape, who made significant contributions to the contemporary landscape of New York City, but have been forgotten over the course of time, has in part been brought to light by institutions such as museums. By engaging with historical narratives and educational articles, our society can form a more inclusive public memory of the past. By doing so is not only acknowledging the profound contributions of these communities to New York City’s cultural fabric, but also giving us power to challenge the mistaken myths. As society reckons with uncomfortable truths from the past, we must adapt with it and strive towards a more inclusive and equal society.
